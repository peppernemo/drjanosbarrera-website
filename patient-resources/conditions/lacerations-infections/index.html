Hand Lacerations & Infections

Cuts, puncture wounds, nail and fingertip infections. Small on the surface, sometimes deeper than they look — prompt care prevents complications.

What are these?

The hand has tendons, nerves, arteries, and joints all packed close to the skin, so even a small cut can hit something important. Hand infections can move fast because the tendon sheaths and deep spaces run throughout the hand. The common ones we see in clinic are:

  • Lacerations — cuts from knives, glass, box cutters, saws
  • Paronychia — infection around the edge of the nail
  • Felon — infection of the fleshy pad of the fingertip
  • Cellulitis — a skin infection with redness and warmth
  • Bite wounds — human or animal bite punctures, particularly high-risk
  • Flexor tenosynovitis — a deep infection inside the tendon sheath of the finger, a surgical emergency

Common symptoms

  • A cut that is bleeding, deep, or on the palm side of a finger
  • Inability to bend or straighten a finger after a cut (may mean a tendon is injured)
  • Numbness in a finger after a cut (a nerve may be cut)
  • Redness, swelling, warmth, and pain around a nail or fingertip
  • Fever or red streaks running up the arm
  • A finger that is painful when gently straightened, swollen all the way around, and held slightly bent (four signs of tendon sheath infection)

Why does it happen?

Lacerations are usually from sharp kitchen or work tools. Paronychia often starts after hangnail pulling or nail biting. Felons develop when bacteria enter through a splinter or tiny cut to the fingertip pad. Bite wounds are high-risk because the teeth inoculate bacteria deep into the hand. Diabetes and immunosuppression raise the risk of all hand infections.

Treatment options

Laceration care

  • Wound washing and closure. Clean cuts can be closed with stitches or skin glue in the office or emergency room. Deep cuts are explored to make sure no tendon, nerve, or artery is injured.
  • Tetanus update. A tetanus shot is given if you have not had one in the last 5 years.
  • Surgical repair. If a tendon or nerve is cut, surgery is scheduled within 1 to 2 weeks.

Infection care

  • Early paronychia. Warm soaks several times a day and oral antibiotics often resolve it.
  • Drainage. If there is pus under the skin or nail, drainage in the office or operating room is needed; antibiotics alone will not cure a collection of pus.
  • IV antibiotics and surgery. Bite wounds, felons that have not drained, and any tendon sheath infection need prompt surgical drainage and IV antibiotics.

What to expect at your visit

Dr. Barrera will examine the injury, test finger movement and sensation, and check for signs of deeper infection. X-rays are taken if there is any chance a piece of glass, metal, or tooth is inside the wound or if there is concern for bone involvement. The treatment plan depends on how deep the injury is and whether anything is cut or infected underneath.

When to seek care right away

Go to an emergency room for heavy bleeding that will not stop with pressure, for a finger that has lost movement or sensation after a cut, for any bite wound, for a red line tracking up the arm, for fever with a hand infection, or for a finger that is swollen, very painful with any motion, and held bent. These can progress quickly.
